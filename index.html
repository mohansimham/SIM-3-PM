Before You Begin

A quick refresher on the frameworks you'll apply in this simulation.

🔍

User Research

Understanding users beyond what they tell you

User research is about uncovering real behaviour, not just stated preferences. Methods like ethnographic observation and Jobs-to-Be-Done (JTBD) help you understand what users are actually trying to accomplish — and why they hire your product to do it.

How to apply it

Ask: What job is the user hiring this product to do? What are they doing right before and after using it? What would they switch to if this didn't exist? Focus on the context of use, not just feature requests.

Example

Netflix discovered users don't just want "more movies" — they want to quickly find something they'll enjoy. The job isn't browsing, it's deciding. This insight drove the recommendation engine.

User Research Plan Template

  1. Research Question — What do we need to learn? Be specific. Not "do users like it?"
  2. Method Selection — Interviews (why), surveys (how many), usability tests (can they), analytics (do they)?
  3. Participant Criteria — Who to recruit? Active users, churned users, non-users? How many?
  4. Discussion Guide — Open-ended questions. No leading. "Tell me about..." not "Do you like..."
  5. Analysis Framework — How will you code findings? Themes, affinity maps, journey maps?
  6. Insight to Action — Each insight maps to a recommendation. No orphan findings.
  7. Stakeholder Readout — Present findings with video clips. Stories > statistics for buy-in.

A/B Testing

Making decisions with data, not opinions

A/B testing compares two versions of something to see which performs better. But good A/B testing is more than just running experiments — it's choosing the right metric, ensuring statistical significance, and knowing when NOT to test.

How to apply it

Ask: What's the primary metric I'm optimising for? Is my sample size large enough? Could this test have unintended second-order effects? Am I testing a meaningful difference or just noise?

Example

Netflix tests everything from thumbnail images to UI layouts. But they learned that optimising for clicks isn't the same as optimising for satisfaction — a clickbait thumbnail gets views but increases churn.

A/B Test Design Template

  1. Hypothesis — "If we [change X], then [metric Y] will [increase/decrease] by [Z%] because [reason]."
  2. Primary Metric — One metric that defines success. Not five. ONE.
  3. Guardrail Metrics — What must NOT get worse? Revenue, retention, load time?
  4. Sample Size & Duration — Use a calculator. How many users? How many days for significance?
  5. Segmentation Plan — New vs returning? Mobile vs desktop? Geography? Plan segments upfront.
  6. Success Criteria — "We ship if primary metric improves by X% at 95% confidence."
  7. Decision Protocol — What if results are inconclusive? What if guardrails are hit?

Product Strategy

Choosing where to play and how to win

Product strategy is about making deliberate choices: which markets to enter, what to build vs. license, how to price, and when to expand. It's the bridge between company vision and the product roadmap.

How to apply it

Ask: Does this move strengthen our core or dilute it? Are we entering a market where we have a right to win? What are we saying no to by saying yes to this? How does this change our competitive position?

Example

Netflix chose to invest billions in original content instead of relying on licensed shows. It was a massive bet — but it reduced supplier power and created a unique library no competitor could replicate.

Strategy Canvas Template

  1. Vision — Where are we going? What does the world look like when we win?
  2. Playing Field — Which market, segment, or job are we competing in?
  3. How to Win — Cost leadership, differentiation, or niche focus? Pick one.
  4. Key Capabilities — What must we be world-class at to execute this strategy?
  5. OKRs — 3 objectives, 3 key results each. Quarterly. Measurable. Ambitious.
  6. What We Won't Do — Strategic clarity comes from what you say no to.

Innovation Management

Knowing when to disrupt yourself before someone else does

Innovation management is about balancing exploitation (improving what works) with exploration (trying what's new). The hardest part is cannibalising your own successful product before a competitor does it for you.

How to apply it

Ask: Is our current model at risk of disruption? What would a startup build to compete with us? Are we investing enough in experiments vs. optimisation? Can we run this innovation without destabilising the core business?

Example

Netflix killed its own profitable DVD business to go all-in on streaming — before anyone forced them to. They disrupted themselves. Should they now do the same with live content and gaming?

Innovation Portfolio Framework

  1. Core (70%) — Incremental improvements to existing products. Low risk, predictable returns.
  2. Adjacent (20%) — New features or markets adjacent to current strengths. Medium risk.
  3. Transformational (10%) — Breakthrough bets that could redefine the category. High risk, high reward.
  4. Kill Criteria — For each bet: what evidence would make us stop? Set upfront.
  5. Review Cadence — Monthly for core, quarterly for adjacent, bi-annual for transformational.
  6. Cannibalisation Test — If we don't disrupt ourselves, who will? When?
